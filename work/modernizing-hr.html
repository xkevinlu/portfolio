Rethinking HR Time

In 2015, ADP opened an Innovations Lab to build their next-gen HR software. By 2017, a new intuitive HR experience was launched, but users hated having to switch back to a legacy system to do other tasks.

I was part of the Innovation Lab's ambitious effort to consumerize HR, unifying 10+ legacy products into a single experience so millions of workers could self-service essential HR tasks.

THE GOAL

"Do" More Than Just Pay

The Self Service MVP lacked a lot of self service.

In its current state, our product was essentially a (successful) pay stub visualizer. Over 80% of our usage was for the Pay feature.

"Great for viewing, not great for doing."

The reality was that employees and managers often had to manage a seperate legacy login to do more complex workflows and HR tasks, such as requesting a leave of absence.

These users were hesitant to upgrade because they couldn't do what they needed to do without having to go into another system anyway.

Therfore, the first step was to identify what employees and managers needed to do and enable them to do it efficiently, improving the flow of work and ultimately driving adoption.

THE APPROACH AND CONSTRAINTS

Consumerize HR Upfront

Our new employee and manager self service (ESS & MSS) was built on a new front end stack, driven by APIs from various disjointed back-end systems. This strategy enabled us to go to market much faster, but also had a tremendous impact on the quality of the experience we could deliver.

DESIGN FOR THE 99%

The strategy was to “consumerize HR” with employee and manager self service in mind, creating new mental models where needed, and optimizing for the mass market rather than a niche group of practitioners.

The goal was to break down product silos, create new universal mental models, and hide the system complexity of various back-end flows. This meant often facing pushback from practitioners, and leaning into our user research to test our hypotheses.

EMBEDDED INTO SCRUM AND CENTRALIZED

Our team had a hybrid of decentralized vs. centralized organization. Each designer was embedded on a product team with a triad, and also frequently met with the entire design team for standups, design reviews, and discussions to ensure a unified experience. 

I worked closely with the Time product owner and engineers to execute features in the current and upcoming sprint, while concurrently working with other designers and stakeholders to plan future work and ensure system consistency.

FRAMING THE PROBLEM

"Do" What?

Many fingers pointed to “feature parity” with legacy systems as a major need, and our PO had pressure to ship features ASAP. Chasing feature parity however wouldn't help us solve user problems any better than today's solution. I used the Jobs To Be Done model to reframe features as benefits for the user, and to think in terms of achieving outcomes through user task analysis.

Feature Job to be Done
Team Timecards (Manager) Manager wants to efficiently approve all their employee's timecards
Schedule & Shift Swap (Employee) Shift employee wants to view and modify their work hours
Total Absence Management Integration (Employee) Employee needs to take time off for family, illness, or child birth

"Do" Where?

Finally, it was important to get a lay of the land, and understand the high level architecture of where users would go to complete certain tasks.

I often paired designed / programmed with an engineer throughout the development cycle. PROCESS

Communicating Design

Working closely with the PM and Lead Dev of each team, I would sketch and whiteboard concepts/flows, then translate them into medium / hi-fidelity wireframes. We had a relatively mature design language with many components implemented in code, so it was relatively straightforward to assemble the visual elements.

For remote and federated teams, I leaned into using InVision Freehand for whiteboarding and collaboration during the brainstorming phases.

I created vision videos and prototypes to help imagine what could be. This was the case when the UX team wanted to explore what could be possible with voice and chatbot technology.
